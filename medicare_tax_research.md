# Medicare Taxes for California Sole Proprietors (2025-2026)
## Research for Self-Employed Individual with Unemployed Spouse

---

## 1. Self-Employment Tax: Medicare Component Breakdown

Self-employed sole proprietors pay **both** the employer and employee portions of FICA taxes via self-employment (SE) tax.

### Total SE Tax Rate: 15.3%
| Component | Employee Share | Employer Share | Total (Self-Employed) |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Social Security | 6.2% | 6.2% | **12.4%** |
| Medicare | 1.45% | 1.45% | **2.9%** |
| **Combined** | 7.65% | 7.65% | **15.3%** |

### Key Caps and Calculations
- **Social Security wage base (2025):** $176,100 -- 12.4% applies only up to this amount
- **Social Security wage base (2026):** $184,500
- **Medicare:** No wage cap -- 2.9% applies to ALL net self-employment earnings
- **Taxable base:** 92.35% of net SE income (the IRS reduces your net earnings by 7.65% before calculating SE tax, to approximate the employer-half exclusion that W-2 employees get)

### Example: $250,000 Net SE Income (2026)
```
Taxable SE income: $250,000 x 92.35% = $230,875
Social Security tax: $184,500 x 12.4% = $22,878.00
Medicare tax: $230,875 x 2.9%        = $6,695.38
Total SE tax:                         = $29,573.38
Deductible half (on 1040):            = $14,786.69
```

## 2. Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% Surtax)

The Affordable Care Act imposed an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax on high earners, effective since 2013. These thresholds are NOT indexed for inflation.

### Thresholds (unchanged since 2013, same for 2025 and 2026)
| Filing Status | Threshold |
|--------------|-----------|
| Married Filing Jointly | **$250,000** |
| Married Filing Separately | **$125,000** |
| Single | **$200,000** |
| Head of Household | **$200,000** |

### How It Works for Self-Employed
- Applies to self-employment income exceeding the threshold
- Calculated on **Schedule SE** and reported on **Form 8959**
- Only the income ABOVE the threshold is taxed at the additional 0.9%
- There is **no employer match** on this additional 0.9% -- the self-employed person pays it alone (same as W-2 employees)
- You **cannot deduct** half of the additional 0.9% tax (unlike the base 2.9%)

### Example: MFJ with $300,000 Net SE Income
```
Base Medicare tax: $300,000 x 92.35% x 2.9% = $8,034.45
Additional Medicare tax: ($300,000 - $250,000) x 0.9% = $450.00
Total Medicare-related tax: $8,484.45
```

### Combined Effective Medicare Rate Above Threshold
- Below threshold: 2.9% (self-employed)
- Above threshold: 2.9% + 0.9% = **3.8%** (self-employed)

## 3. Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) -- 3.8%

### What It Is
A 3.8% surtax on net investment income, also from the ACA (since 2013).

### Thresholds (same as Additional Medicare Tax)
| Filing Status | MAGI Threshold |
|--------------|---------------|
| Married Filing Jointly | **$250,000** |
| Single / HoH | **$200,000** |
| Married Filing Separately | **$125,000** |

### Critical Point for Sole Proprietors
**Active self-employment income is EXEMPT from the 3.8% NIIT.** The NIIT applies to:
- Interest, dividends, capital gains
- Rental and royalty income (if passive)
- Passive business income
- Annuities

It does **NOT** apply to:
- Wages
- Self-employment income from an active trade or business
- Active S-corp/partnership income

### How It Is Calculated
The 3.8% tax applies to the **lesser of**:
1. Net investment income, OR
2. The amount by which MAGI exceeds the threshold

### When It Matters for a Sole Proprietor
If you sell a business property for capital gains, that gain IS subject to NIIT (it's investment income, not active SE income). So selling a $500K property could trigger both:
- Additional Medicare Tax (0.9%) on your SE income above $250K
- NIIT (3.8%) on the capital gain portion, if MAGI exceeds $250K

## 4. Unemployed Spouse -- How They Factor In

### Filing Jointly
When filing MFJ, the $250,000 threshold for both the Additional Medicare Tax and NIIT is based on **combined** income. Having an unemployed spouse with zero income does not change the threshold -- it remains $250,000 combined.

### Medicare Coverage for the Unemployed Spouse
**Self-employment tax payments do NOT provide Medicare coverage to a spouse.** Medicare is an individual benefit. However:

- **Premium-Free Part A:** The unemployed spouse CAN qualify for premium-free Medicare Part A based on the working spouse's record IF:
  - The working spouse has at least **40 quarters (10 years)** of Medicare-covered employment
  - The working spouse is at least **age 62**
  - They have been **married at least 1 year**
  - The unemployed spouse is **age 65 or older**

- **If requirements are NOT met:** The spouse would need to pay the Part A premium:
  - With 30-39 quarters: **$311/month** (2026)
  - With fewer than 30 quarters: **$505/month** (2026)

- **Part B:** Both spouses must each enroll separately and each pay their own Part B premium ($202.90/month standard in 2026)

- **No family plan:** Medicare does NOT offer family coverage. Each person has their own individual Medicare enrollment.

### Premium Deduction for Spouse
A self-employed sole proprietor CAN deduct Medicare premiums paid for their spouse as part of the **Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction** (Schedule 1, Line 17). This includes:
- Spouse's Part B premiums
- Spouse's Part D premiums
- Spouse's Medigap/Medicare Advantage premiums
- **Limitation:** Deduction cannot exceed net SE income

## 5. Deductions Available

### A. Deductible Half of SE Tax (Above-the-Line)
- You can deduct **50% of your total self-employment tax** as an adjustment to income on Form 1040, Schedule 1
- This reduces your AGI (and thus your MAGI for IRMAA purposes)
- This applies to the base 15.3% SE tax only -- NOT the additional 0.9% Medicare surtax
- Claimed on **Schedule 1, Line 15**

### B. Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction
- Deduct Medicare premiums (Part B, Part D, Medigap) for yourself, spouse, and dependents
- This is an **above-the-line** deduction (Schedule 1, Line 17) -- no need to itemize
- The 7.5% AGI floor does NOT apply (unlike the Schedule A medical deduction)
- **Cannot exceed** your net self-employment earned income
- **Cannot claim** for months where either spouse was eligible for employer-subsidized health coverage

### C. What You CANNOT Deduct
- The additional 0.9% Medicare surtax -- not deductible at all
- The 3.8% NIIT -- not deductible
- SE tax itself is not deductible for SE tax calculation purposes (circular)

## 6. California-Specific Considerations

### No State-Level Medicare Tax
California does **not** impose any separate state Medicare tax. All Medicare taxes are federal.

### California State Income Tax on SE Income
Self-employment income is subject to California state income tax at progressive rates:
- Rates range from **1% to 12.3%**
- Additional **1% Mental Health Services Tax** on taxable income over $1,000,000
- Effective top marginal rate: **13.3%**

### California SDI (State Disability Insurance)
- SDI rate for 2025: **1.2%**; for 2026: **1.3%**
- As of 2024, there is **no wage cap** on SDI (SB 951 removed it)
- Self-employed individuals are NOT automatically covered but can **opt in** through the DIEC program
- SDI provides short-term disability and Paid Family Leave benefits

### California Individual Health Care Mandate
- California requires health insurance coverage or you pay a penalty
- 2025 penalty: **$950 per adult, $475 per child** (or 2.5% of household income above filing threshold, whichever is greater)
- Medicare enrollment satisfies this mandate

### No State FICA Equivalent
California does not have a state-level Social Security or Medicare equivalent tax. The payroll taxes California does impose (SDI, ETT, SUI) generally apply to employers with employees, not sole proprietors without employees.

## 7. Medicare PREMIUMS -- Self-Employed vs. Employed

### Key Insight: Premiums Are the Same for Everyone
There is **NO difference** in Medicare premium amounts based on employment status. Self-employed and employed individuals pay identical premiums. The differences are only in tax treatment.

## 8. Selling a $500K Business Property -- IRMAA Impact

### The 2-Year Look-Back Problem
IRMAA uses your MAGI from **2 years prior**. So:
- A property sale in **2024** affects **2026** premiums
- A property sale in **2025** affects **2027** premiums
- A property sale in **2026** affects **2028** premiums

### How Capital Gains Enter MAGI
Capital gains from selling business/investment property are included in MAGI. Unlike a primary residence, there is **no $250K/$500K exclusion** for business/investment property.

### Example Scenario: $500K Business Property Sale
Assume:
- Purchase price (basis): $200,000
- Depreciation recapture: $80,000
- Capital gain: $300,000 ($500K - $200K)
- Normal SE income: $200,000
- Filing MFJ

```
Normal MAGI:                    ~$200,000 (under IRMAA threshold)
MAGI in year of sale:           ~$200,000 + $300,000 = ~$500,000

Normal IRMAA:                   $0 (below $218,000 MFJ threshold)
IRMAA in affected year:         Tier 4 ($410,001 - $749,999 MFJ)
  Part B surcharge per person:  $446.30/month = $5,355.60/year
  Part D surcharge per person:  $83.30/month  = $999.60/year
  Per person total:             $6,355.20/year
  For couple:                   $12,710.40/year
```

### Tax Stack on the $300K Capital Gain
| Tax | Rate | Amount |
|-----|------|--------|
| Federal capital gains (15% or 20%) | 15-20% | $45,000 - $60,000 |
| Depreciation recapture (25% on $80K) | 25% | $20,000 |
| Net Investment Income Tax | 3.8% | $11,400 |
| California state tax | ~9-13.3% | $27,000 - $39,900 |
| IRMAA surcharge (couple, 1 year) | -- | $12,710 |
| **Approximate total tax burden on gain** | | **$116,110 - $143,010** |

### Can You Appeal? (Form SSA-44)
The SSA allows IRMAA appeals via **Form SSA-44** for qualifying "life-changing events":
- Retirement or work stoppage
- Death of a spouse
- Divorce
- Loss of income-producing property (involuntary -- disaster, fraud)
- Marriage

**A voluntary sale of business property for profit generally does NOT qualify as a life-changing event.** The SSA considers this realized income, not a life-changing event. However, if the sale was accompanied by retirement or work cessation, you may have grounds to appeal on that basis.

### Strategies to Mitigate IRMAA Impact
1. **Installment sale:** Spread the gain over multiple years to stay in lower IRMAA tiers
2. **1031 Exchange:** Defer capital gains entirely by exchanging into like-kind property
3. **Time the sale:** If possible, sell in a year when other income is low
4. **Qualified Opportunity Zone investment:** Defer and potentially reduce capital gains
5. **Charitable giving:** Donate appreciated property or use a Donor-Advised Fund to offset gain
6. **Deduction stacking:** Maximize above-the-line deductions in the sale year (retirement contributions, SE health insurance, half of SE tax)

## Summary: Total Medicare-Related Tax Burden for High-Earning CA Sole Proprietor (MFJ)

For a sole proprietor earning $300K with an unemployed spouse:

| Item | Annual Cost |
|------|------------|
| Medicare portion of SE tax (2.9% on 92.35% of $300K) | $8,034 |
| Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% on $50K over $250K) | $450 |
| Part B premiums (2 people x $202.90 x 12) | $4,870 |
| Part D premiums (2 people x ~$46.50 x 12) | $1,116 |
| IRMAA surcharges (if MAGI $274K-$342K tier, 2 people) | $5,770 |
| **Total annual Medicare-related costs** | **~$20,240** |

*The deductible half of SE tax ($4,017) reduces your AGI, partially offsetting these costs.*
