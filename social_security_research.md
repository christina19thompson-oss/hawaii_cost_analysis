# Social Security Benefits Research: Parents' Scenario

**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Scenario:** Dad (80), Mom (69), married, Dad is CA sole proprietor considering selling $500K business property and retiring

---

## 1. Social Security for Self-Employed Sole Proprietors

### What Counts as "Earnings"

Social Security counts **net earnings from self-employment (NESE)** -- not gross revenue. This is calculated from Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business) and Schedule SE (Self-Employment Tax).

- **NESE = Net profit from Schedule C x 92.35%** (the 92.35% factor accounts for the employer-equivalent portion of SE tax)
- The self-employment tax rate is **15.3%** (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare)
- As a sole proprietor, Dad pays both the "employer" and "employee" shares
- He can deduct the employer-equivalent portion (7.65%) on his 1040

### How Credits/Quarters Are Earned

| Year | Earnings per Credit | Max 4 Credits Requires |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 2025 | $1,810 | $7,240 |
| 2026 | $1,890 | $7,560 |

- **40 credits (10 years of work) = eligible for retirement benefits**
- Maximum of 4 credits per year
- If Dad has worked 10+ years as a sole proprietor reporting SE income, he qualifies

### Benefit Calculation (AIME/PIA)

Social Security uses the **highest 35 years of indexed earnings** to calculate:
1. **AIME** (Average Indexed Monthly Earnings) -- average of top 35 years, indexed for inflation
2. **PIA** (Primary Insurance Amount) -- the benefit at Full Retirement Age, calculated via a progressive formula with "bend points"

If Dad had fewer than 35 high-earning years, zeros fill in, reducing the average. Self-employed income that was underreported or not reported means lower benefits.

## 2. Dad at Age 80: Options and Delayed Retirement Credits

### Full Retirement Age (FRA)

For someone born ~1946 (now 80), FRA was **66 years old**.

### If He's Already Collecting

If Dad started collecting at 66 (FRA), he's been receiving his full PIA for 14 years, plus annual COLAs. No action needed except to understand the COLA-adjusted amount.

### If He Delayed to 70

- **Delayed retirement credits (DRCs):** 8% increase per year from FRA to age 70
- From 66 to 70 = **32% increase** over his PIA
- DRCs stop accumulating at age 70 -- there is NO benefit to delaying past 70

### If He NEVER Filed (Critical Scenario)

**If Dad is 80 and has never claimed Social Security, he has been leaving money on the table for 10 years.** Key facts:

- **Retroactive benefits are limited to 6 months maximum** -- SSA will NOT pay back to age 70
- If he files today at 80, he gets:
  - A lump sum covering the last 6 months (approximately $X times 6)
  - Going forward, his monthly benefit at the age-70 rate (FRA + 32% DRCs) plus all COLAs from age 70 to present
  - **He CANNOT recover the ~9.5 years of unclaimed benefits from age 70 to 79.5**
- **This is potentially $200,000+ in permanently lost benefits** (rough estimate: ~$2,500/mo x 114 months)

**ACTION ITEM: If Dad has not yet filed, he should file IMMEDIATELY. Every month of delay past 70 is pure loss.**

### 2025-2026 Maximum Monthly Benefits

| Claiming Age | 2025 Maximum | 2026 Maximum |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Age 62 | $2,831 | $2,969 |
| Age 67 (FRA) | $4,018 | $4,108 |
| Age 70 | $5,108 | $5,181 |

**Note:** These maximums require 35 years of maximum taxable earnings. A sole proprietor with moderate income would receive significantly less. The **average** SS benefit in 2025 is approximately $1,976/month.

## 3. Spousal Benefits for Mom (Age 69)

### Eligibility

- Mom **CAN** receive spousal benefits even with NO work history of her own
- Requirements: married at least 1 year, spouse (Dad) must be collecting or eligible for benefits
- She must be at least 62 (she's 69, so she qualifies)

### Mom's FRA

Born ~1957: Her FRA is **66 years and 6 months**. At age 69, she is **past her FRA**.

### Benefit Amount

- **Maximum spousal benefit = 50% of Dad's PIA** (his FRA benefit, NOT his delayed/enhanced benefit)
- Since Mom is past her FRA, she gets the full 50% -- no reduction
- **Spousal benefits do NOT earn delayed retirement credits** -- there is no benefit for Mom to wait past her FRA
- If Mom has not filed, she should also file immediately

### Example Calculation

If Dad's PIA is $2,000/month:
- Mom's spousal benefit = **$1,000/month**
- Combined household = **$3,000/month** (plus Dad's DRCs if he delayed)

If Dad delayed to 70, his check might be $2,640/month (PIA + 32%), but Mom's spousal benefit is still based on the $2,000 PIA = $1,000/month.

**Combined household if Dad delayed to 70: ~$3,640/month**

### If Mom Has Any Work History

If Mom has even minimal work credits of her own, SSA will pay the higher of:
- Her own retirement benefit, OR
- The spousal benefit (50% of Dad's PIA)

She does NOT get both.

## 4. Selling the $500K Business Property -- Impact on Social Security

### Capital Gains and the Earnings Test

**Capital gains from selling a business property do NOT count as "earnings" for the Social Security earnings test.**

The SSA only counts:
- Wages from employment
- Net earnings from self-employment

The following do NOT count:
- Capital gains (including from sale of business property)
- Investment income (dividends, interest, rental income)
- Pensions, annuities, IRA distributions

### Structure of the Sale Matters

- **Asset/property sale (capital gain):** Does NOT affect SS benefits
- **Consulting/non-compete agreements (ordinary income):** DOES count as earnings
- **Earn-out provisions tied to future work:** May count as SE income

### Tax Impact (Separate from SS Benefits)

While capital gains don't affect the earnings test, the $500K gain WILL:
1. **Potentially make SS benefits more taxable at the federal level** -- up to 85% of SS benefits become taxable when combined income exceeds $44,000 (married filing jointly)
2. **Trigger federal capital gains tax** (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on bracket)
3. **Trigger California state income tax** on the capital gain (CA taxes capital gains as ordinary income, up to 13.3%)
4. **Potentially trigger Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)** of 3.8% if MAGI exceeds $250,000 (MFJ)

### Estimated Tax on $500K Property Sale

Rough estimates (consult a CPA for exact figures):
- Federal capital gains (15-20%): $75,000 - $100,000
- California state tax (up to 13.3%): ~$50,000 - $66,500
- Possible NIIT (3.8%): ~$19,000
- **Total tax burden could be $144,000 - $185,000+ on $500K gain**

Consider: Section 1031 exchange (if reinvesting in like-kind property), installment sale to spread gains, or opportunity zone investment.

## 5. Earnings Test at Age 80

### The Earnings Test Does NOT Apply to Dad

**The earnings test stops at Full Retirement Age.** Since Dad's FRA was 66 and he is now 80:

- He can earn unlimited income from self-employment with ZERO reduction in SS benefits
- The earnings test only applies to people collecting SS benefits BEFORE reaching FRA
- This has been true since he turned 66

**Bottom line: Dad's ongoing sole proprietor income has no effect on his SS benefit amount.**

However, if Dad continues to have high-earning years, SSA automatically recalculates his benefit annually. If a current year's earnings are higher than one of the 35 years used in his AIME calculation, his benefit could actually **increase** slightly.

## 6. Survivor Benefits -- If Dad Passes

### What Mom Would Receive

- **100% of Dad's actual benefit amount** (including DRCs and COLAs) if she claims at her FRA or later
- Since Mom is already 69 (past her FRA of 66.5), she would receive **the full survivor benefit**
- She would receive the **higher of**: her own benefit (spousal or retirement) OR the survivor benefit
- She does NOT get both -- the survivor benefit replaces the spousal benefit

### Key Details

- Mom must have been married to Dad for at least 9 months at time of death
- If Mom remarries before age 60, she loses eligibility (remarriage after 60 preserves it)
- If Dad was receiving a reduced benefit (claimed early), the survivor benefit may be limited to the greater of:
  - Dad's reduced benefit amount, OR
  - 82.5% of Dad's PIA

### Practical Impact

If Dad's benefit is $2,640/month (with DRCs):
- While both alive: Dad gets $2,640 + Mom gets $1,000 (spousal) = **$3,640/month**
- After Dad passes: Mom gets $2,640 (survivor) = **$2,640/month**
- **Household income drops by ~27%** -- important for financial planning

### One-Time Lump Sum Death Benefit

SSA pays a **$255 one-time death benefit** to the surviving spouse. (Yes, it's still $255 -- hasn't been updated since 1954.)

## 8. California-Specific: State Tax on Social Security

### California does NOT tax Social Security benefits.

- **100% exempt** from California state income tax
- Applies to all types: retirement, spousal, disability, survivor benefits
- No income threshold -- exempt regardless of total income
- This is one of the major tax advantages of living in California as a retiree

### However, California DOES Tax:

- Pension income (fully taxable as ordinary income)
- IRA/401(k) distributions
- Capital gains (taxed as ordinary income -- up to 13.3%)
- Wages and self-employment income

### Federal Taxation of Social Security

Combined income = AGI + nontaxable interest + 1/2 of SS benefits

| Filing Status | Combined Income | SS Taxable Portion |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| MFJ | < $32,000 | 0% |
| MFJ | $32,000 - $44,000 | Up to 50% |
| MFJ | > $44,000 | Up to 85% |

**In the year they sell the $500K property, up to 85% of their SS benefits will be federally taxable** due to the spike in income.

## 9. Retroactive Payments if Dad Hasn't Been Claiming

### The Critical Question: Has Dad Filed?

**If Dad is 80 and has NEVER filed for Social Security:**

| Scenario | Outcome |
|----------|---------|
| Files now at 80 | Gets 6-month retroactive lump sum + going-forward monthly payments |
| Retroactive period | Maximum 6 months back from filing date (back to ~age 79.5) |
| Benefit rate | His age-70 rate (PIA + 32% DRCs) + all COLAs since age 70 |
| Lost benefits | ~9.5 years of monthly payments are permanently lost |
| Estimated loss | Potentially $200,000 - $400,000+ depending on his PIA |

### Why Only 6 Months?

SSA limits retroactive benefits to a maximum of 6 months for anyone past FRA. This is a hard rule -- no exceptions, no appeals.

### The 6-Month Retroactive Catch

Taking the 6-month retroactive payment has a subtle cost: the retroactive start date becomes your official benefit start date, which can slightly reduce your ongoing monthly benefit (you lose 6 months of DRCs). However, since Dad is past 70, DRCs are already maxed -- **there is no cost to taking the 6-month retroactive at his age. He should absolutely take it.**

### Spousal Benefits Retroactivity

If Mom also hasn't filed, she may also be eligible for up to 6 months of retroactive spousal benefits. **Both should file simultaneously and immediately.**
